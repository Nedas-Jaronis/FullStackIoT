IoT · Computer Vision · Assistive Wearable
GlassTint
Glasses that tell you who you can trust.
An assistive wearable for people with visual or memory impairment. A quiet button press on the frame captures a face, a cloud-hosted ArcFace model verifies it, and the wearer hears who it is — along with the role they play in their life. Designed to protect against strangers, scammers, and the everyday confusion of forgetting a face.
View on GitHub
The problem
Why faces at the door are a daily risk for the people we built this for.
🦯
Visual impairment
For people who are blind or have significant vision loss, every face is an unrecognizable blur — or invisible entirely. A family member and a stranger at the door look the same, forcing reliance on a voice that anyone can fake.
⟳
Memory impairment
People with dementia or cognitive decline lose the ability to place familiar faces. A caregiver this morning can feel like a complete stranger by afternoon.
⚠
Scams and bad actors
Vulnerable people are disproportionately targeted by impersonators, fake caregivers, and scammers exploiting confusion. Confirming identity protects them from giving up information, money, or access they shouldn't.
How GlassTint helps
One press, two seconds, spoken result — plus the context that actually matters.
◉
Trusted or unknown
Press the frame button when someone approaches. The system says "Anthony — brother" or "Unknown face" through the bone-conduction audio, privately.
✎
Roles and context
Each enrolled person has a short description — "daughter", "home nurse Monday/Wednesday", "pharmacy delivery". The wearer gets a real cue, not just a name they might not remember either.
⌂
Admin dashboard
Family or a caregiver manages the trust list from this very web UI — add a person, upload 3–5 photos, set their role. Removing a former contact is just as fast, which matters when a relationship changes.
This version, and where it's headed
The demo solves the core problem. Here's what comes next.
▶
Today: on-demand verify
A press-to-capture model. Privacy-first — the camera never streams and nothing leaves the glasses without the wearer's intent. Round trip is under two seconds on Cloud Run.
∞
Next: always-on recognition
A future build can run continuous low-rate capture, announcing people as they approach — no button required. Useful indoors where the wearer expects familiar faces and wants to be forewarned.
◆
Beyond: liveness + alerts
Liveness detection to block photo-spoof attacks, stranger-at-the-door alerts to a family member's phone, and offline fallback when connectivity drops.
How it works under the hood
Two microcontrollers talk over ESP-NOW; the vision MCU talks to a Cloud Run gateway over HTTPS.
System overview — button-to-audio round-trip across MCU-B, MCU-A, and the Cloud Run gateway.
MCU-A · Vision & network — Freenove ESP32-WROVER with an OV3660 camera. Captures a JPEG on trigger, POSTs it to the gateway over HTTPS, and sends the result back to MCU-B via ESP-NOW.
MCU-B · User I/O — ESP32 Dev board: trigger button, status LED, DAC audio to the speaker amplifier, LiPo battery with voltage monitoring.
